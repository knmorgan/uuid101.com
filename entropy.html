Entropy Analysis

All UUIDs are 128 bits, but they have varying amounts of entropy. For example, a version 1 UUID has a 60-bit timestamp and a 48-bit node ID, but it cannot be said to have 108 bits of entropy. First, the node ID only has 47 bits of entropy rather than 48, due to the multicast bit on MAC addresses and the fixed bit on random node IDs. And the timestamp is using an epoch date in the 16th century, and can address dates into the 53rd century, which is not going to happen in practice. Over any given century, the timestamp used in a version 1 UUID can be said to have ~55 bits of entropy. And likewise, it has ~48 bits of entropy over any given decade. When used over the course of a century, a version 1 UUID would have approximately 102 bits of entropy in total.

Version 4 UUIDs are simpler. In general, six total bits are consumed by the version and variant markers, leaving 122 bits of entropy. This means that, over a century, roughly one million times as many version 4 UUIDs can be expressed than version 1 UUIDs.

Now, despite this, version 1 UUIDs should guarantee uniqueness by their nature of specifying a point in time and space, assuming they're using a MAC address rather than a randomly-generated node ID. There is a chance of collision among version 4 UUIDs, although it is negligibly small.
